# E5 Belief-Driven Copywriting Method

## Core Philosophy

Arguments sell, not words. Every purchase is preceded by a set of beliefs the buyer must hold. The copywriter's job is to engineer those beliefs through structured argumentation, not clever wordplay.

## The Belief Journey

```
Current State ──> Journey ──> Destination ──> Purchase
(Pain/Problem)   (Mechanism)  (Desired Outcome) (Your Offer)
```

1. **Current State**: Where the prospect is now — their frustrations, failed attempts, skepticism
2. **Journey**: The path from problem to solution — your unique mechanism
3. **Destination**: The specific outcome they want — painted vividly
4. **Purchase**: The logical next step once beliefs are in place

## Necessary Beliefs Document

Before writing any script, enumerate the beliefs the viewer must hold to buy. Format each as an "I believe that..." statement.

### Categories

| Category | Example |
|----------|---------|
| **Problem belief** | "I believe my current approach isn't working" |
| **Mechanism belief** | "I believe this specific method can solve it" |
| **Proof belief** | "I believe real people have gotten results" |
| **Offer belief** | "I believe this product delivers the mechanism" |
| **Urgency belief** | "I believe I need to act now" |
| **Trust belief** | "I believe this brand/person is credible" |

### Structure

```json
{
  "beliefs": [
    {
      "id": "B1",
      "category": "problem",
      "statement": "I believe that generic skincare doesn't address MY specific issue",
      "priority": "must-have",
      "addressed_in_scene": 2
    }
  ]
}
```

- **must-have**: Without this belief, no purchase happens
- **nice-to-have**: Strengthens conviction but isn't blocking
- Each script targets 1-3 beliefs (not all at once)

## Unique Mechanism

The proprietary explanation for WHY your solution works differently. It answers: "Why is this different from everything else I've tried?"

Characteristics of a strong mechanism:
- Named and specific (not generic "our formula")
- Explains the HOW, not just the WHAT
- Creates a new category in the viewer's mind
- Makes competing approaches feel outdated

Example: "Our CeraSync process matches ceramide ratios to your skin's pH" vs "Our moisturizer hydrates skin"

## Script Structure

Every belief-driven UGC script follows this 6-part structure:

### 1. Hook (first 2-4 seconds)
- Pattern interrupt — stop the scroll
- Target ONE belief directly
- Must feel native to platform (not polished/ad-like)

**Hook Techniques:**

| Technique | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Visual Pattern Interrupt** | Unexpected image, action, or context that contradicts viewer expectation — the visual itself stops the scroll before they even hear a word | Product in bizarre setting, person mid-unusual-action, jarring close-up |
| **Verbal Callout** | Direct address to the target audience by identity or struggle | "Busy moms — stop scrolling" |
| **Bold Claim** | Definitive assertion that demands attention | "Nobody talks about this ingredient" |
| **Confession** | Vulnerability that builds instant trust | "I was wrong about skincare for 10 years" |
| **Question** | Opens an information gap the viewer needs closed | "Why does your moisturizer stop working after 2 weeks?" |

**Visual Pattern Interrupt** is the most effective hook type for short-form UGC. The viewer decides to keep watching based on what they SEE in the first frame — audio is secondary. Pair a strong visual interrupt with a verbal callout for maximum scroll-stopping power.

### 2. Problem (4-8 seconds)
- Agitate the current state
- Show you understand their specific frustration
- Use their language, not marketing speak
- Create an information gap

### 3. Mechanism (8-15 seconds)
- Introduce the unique mechanism
- Explain WHY it works (not just that it works)
- Keep it simple — one core idea
- Visual demonstration if possible

### 4. Proof (4-8 seconds)
- Evidence the mechanism delivers
- Before/after, testimonial, data point, personal result
- Specific > vague ("in 14 days" > "quickly")
- Show, don't just tell

### 5. Offer (4-6 seconds)
- What they get and what it costs
- Frame value vs price
- Remove risk (guarantee, trial)
- Keep it clean — one clear offer

### 6. CTA (2-4 seconds)
- Single clear action
- Urgency if authentic (limited, seasonal, etc.)
- Repeat the mechanism or destination briefly
- "Link in bio" / "Tap below" / "Use code..."

## Duration-Based Word Limits

| Scene Duration | Word Count | Pacing |
|---------------|------------|--------|
| 4 seconds | 8-12 words | Punchy, one sentence |
| 6 seconds | 12-18 words | Conversational beat |
| 8 seconds | 15-25 words | Full thought, natural pace |
| 12 seconds | 25-35 words | Room for demo + voiceover |

**Total script targets:**
- 15s ad: 35-50 words
- 30s ad: 70-100 words
- 45s ad: 100-140 words
- 60s ad: 140-180 words

## Writing Guidelines

### Voice
- First person, conversational
- Sound like a real person sharing a discovery, not a spokesperson
- Match the avatar's vocabulary level and speech patterns
- Contractions always (it's, don't, I've)

### Tone by Segment
| Segment | Tone | Example Phrasing |
|---------|------|-------------------|
| Beauty/Skincare | Excited discovery | "okay so I finally found..." |
| Health/Wellness | Calm authority | "here's what nobody tells you about..." |
| Tech/SaaS | Problem-solver | "I was wasting hours on X until..." |
| Finance | Relatable frustration | "I used to think investing was..." |
| Food/Bev | Sensory enthusiasm | "this literally changed my morning..." |

### Tactical Tips
- Lead with the viewer's internal monologue, not your product
- One belief per scene maximum
- Specificity > superlatives ("87% saw results in 2 weeks" > "amazing results")
- Objection handling belongs in mechanism or proof, not as a separate section
- End on transformation, not features
- Never open with the brand name
- Avoid "um" and filler in scripts — Veo renders them as awkward pauses

## Quick Reference Checklist

Before finalizing any script, verify:

- [ ] Which belief(s) does this script target?
- [ ] Does the hook stop the scroll in under 3 seconds?
- [ ] Is the mechanism named and explained simply?
- [ ] Is there at least one concrete proof point?
- [ ] Does the CTA have a single clear action?
- [ ] Is word count within limits for each scene duration?
- [ ] Does it sound like a real person, not an ad?
- [ ] Would this feel native on TikTok/Instagram/YouTube Shorts?
